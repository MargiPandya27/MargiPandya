    Generative Models

    Generative models aim to learn the underlying distribution of each class. The goal is to determine the probability of generating an image from the joint distribution p(x, y), particularly when y = 1 or y = -1.

    Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)

    GANs are explicit generative models. The core idea is to learn the distribution of the data without explicitly modeling it, using a parameterized model pmodel(θ) to approximate the real data distribution.

    A normally distributed random variable z is passed through the generator to produce fake images that resemble real images from the original dataset. The training goal is to optimize a zero-sum loss function so that the generator becomes good enough to fool the discriminator into classifying generated images as real.

    The generator G is trained so that the discriminator misclassifies the generated sample x̂ as class 1, i.e., it becomes indistinguishable from real samples x ∼ pdata and generated samples x̂ ∼ pG.

    minG maxD [ Ex ∼ pdata[log D(x)] + Ez ∼ pz[log(1 − D(G(z)))] ]
    • O1: maxD Ex ∼ pdata[log D(x)]
      Train discriminator D to maximize the log probability of correctly classifying real samples.
    • O2: minG Ez ∼ pz[log(1 − D(G(z)))]
      Train generator G to maximize the log probability that the discriminator misclassifies generated samples as real.

    Training Strategy

    When starting with a pretrained discriminator, it might become too confident in identifying fake images. In such a case, log(1 − D(G(z))) becomes log(1 − 0) = 0, leading to no gradient for the generator to learn from.

    Therefore, it is essential to train both the generator and discriminator alternately on different mini-batches using the following objective:

    minG maxD [ Ex ∼ pdata[log D(x)] + Ez ∼ pz[log(1 − D(G(z)))] ]
