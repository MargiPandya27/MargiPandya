Variational Autoencoder (VAE): Likelihood Estimation

Overview

A Variational Autoencoder (VAE) is a generative model that learns a probabilistic mapping between observed data and a latent space. VAEs are widely used for generating complex data such as images, text, and audio.

Motivation

Given a dataset (e.g., faces), a VAE learns to encode each data point into a latent representation. By sampling from this latent space and decoding, the model can generate new, realistic samples.

For data \( X \), the goal is to maximize the marginal likelihood:
\[ p(x) = \int p(x, z) \, dz \] where \( z \) is the latent variable.

This integral is generally intractable, so we approximate it using sampling techniques.

Sampling Techniques

1. Monte Carlo Sampling

Approximate the integral by averaging over samples from the latent space:

\[ p(x) \approx \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} p(x, z^{(i)}), \quad z^{(i)} \sim \text{Uniform} \]

2. Importance Sampling

Sample \( z \) from a proposal distribution \( q(z) \) that is closer to the true posterior:

\[ p(x) = \int \frac{p(x, z)}{q(z)} q(z) \, dz = \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)}\left[\frac{p(x, z)}{q(z)}\right] \] \[ \approx \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{p(x, z^{(i)})}{q(z^{(i)})}, \quad z^{(i)} \sim q(z) \]

Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO)

The log-likelihood can be lower-bounded using Jensen's inequality:

\[ \log p(x) = \log \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \frac{p(x, z)}{q(z)} \right] \geq \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q(z)} \left[ \log \frac{p(x, z)}{q(z)} \right] \]

This lower bound is called the ELBO:

\[ \mathcal{L}(x; \theta, \phi) = \mathbb{E}_{z \sim q_\phi(z|x)} \left[ \log p_\theta(x|z) \right] - D_{KL}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z)) \]

Equality holds when \( q(z) = p(z|x) \), i.e., the approximate posterior matches the true posterior.

VAE Architecture

  • Encoder: Learns \( q_\phi(z|x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu(x), \sigma^2(x)I) \) via a neural network.
  • Decoder: Models \( p_\theta(x|z) \), typically as a Gaussian or Bernoulli distribution.
  • Prior: Usually \( p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I) \).

For images: Encoders use convolutional layers; decoders use transposed convolutions.

KL Divergence (Gaussian Case)

For \( q(z) = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2 I) \), \( p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I) \):
\[ D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^d \left( -\log \sigma_j^2 + \mu_j^2 + \sigma_j^2 - 1 \right) \]

Reparameterization Trick

To enable backpropagation through stochastic sampling, we use:

\[ z = \mu + \sigma \cdot \epsilon, \quad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I) \]

Training & Generation

  1. Encode \( x \) to get \( \mu, \sigma \).
  2. Sample \( z \) via reparameterization.
  3. Decode \( z \) to reconstruct \( x \).
  4. Optimize ELBO: maximize reconstruction, minimize KL divergence.
Generation: Sample \( z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I) \), decode to get a new sample \( x_{\text{new}} \).

Summary

  • VAEs maximize a lower bound (ELBO) on data likelihood.
  • They use neural networks to approximate the intractable posterior.
  • The reparameterization trick enables efficient training.
  • KL divergence regularizes the latent space.
