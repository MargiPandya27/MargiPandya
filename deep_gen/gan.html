    Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)

    GANs are a class of generative models where a generator \( G \) and a discriminator \( D \) are trained in a two-player minimax game. The goal is for \( G \) to generate data that is indistinguishable from real data, and for \( D \) to distinguish between real and generated (fake) data.

    Objective Function

    The overall loss function is given by:

    \[ \min_G \max_D \left( \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}(x)} [\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z(z)} [\log(1 - D(G(z)))] \right) \]

    The two components of this loss correspond to:

    • Discriminator loss:
      \[ \max_D \, \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}(x)}[\log D(x)] \]
      Train \( D \) to correctly classify real data \( x \sim p_{\text{data}} \).
    • Generator loss:
      \[ \min_G \, \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z(z)}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))] \]
      Train \( G \) to generate samples \( G(z) \) such that \( D \) classifies them as real.

    Training Strategy

    If the discriminator is pretrained or initially too strong, it might perfectly classify fake samples, making \( \log(1 - D(G(z))) = \log(1 - 0) = 0 \), resulting in no useful gradient for the generator. Hence, it is crucial to train both \( G \) and \( D \) iteratively over different mini-batches.

    GAN Generation Pipeline

    1. Sample noise vector \( z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I) \)
    2. Generate fake sample \( \hat{x} = G(z) \)
    3. Discriminator receives both real \( x \) and fake \( \hat{x} \), and learns to assign high probability to real and low to fake
    4. Generator is updated to maximize the probability of discriminator misclassifying \( \hat{x} \) as real
